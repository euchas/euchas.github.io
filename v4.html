The Cover Story · Nature Geoscience, July 2024

Water frost on Martian mountaintops

Every morning, a whisper-thin layer of water frost forms on the summits of the Solar System's largest volcanoes — then vanishes by mid-morning. That discovery, led from high-resolution orbital colour imaging, overturned the assumption that Mars's equatorial highlands were too warm for frost.

The finding recast part of the modern Martian water cycle and pointed to exchange between the surface and atmosphere that models had missed. It was chosen for the cover of Nature Geoscience and covered live on CNN and the BBC.

It followed a second headline result — the detection of ferrihydrite in Martian red dust, a mineral that records ancient cold and wet conditions, published in Nature Communications and reaching roughly half a million viewers through a Planetary Society interview.

“A century-old question — why is Mars red — turns out to hold a clue to the planet's watery, potentially habitable past.”— on the ferrihydrite discovery

About

Adomas Valantinas is a planetary scientist and SNSF Postdoc.Mobility Fellow at ETH Zurich, joining the University of Cambridge as an incoming Fellow of the Leverhulme Centre for Life in the Universe. He studies the geological and climatic evolution of Mars — aqueous alteration, iron-oxide mineralogy, and the habitability of ancient crust.

He earned his PhD in Astronomy & Physics at the University of Bern, with earlier degrees from Copenhagen and Vilnius, and held a fellowship at Brown University. He contributes to ESA's CaSSIS, NASA's HiRISE, and the Perseverance rover's Mastcam-Z, and does analog fieldwork in Iceland and the Azores.

He is also a radio host and science communicator on NTS.live and RadioVilnius.live, and has collaborated with artists to bring planetary science to new audiences.

Beyond the Lab

Valantinas's Mars discoveries have travelled well beyond academic journals. His work has been reported by the Associated Press, CNN, the BBC, The Guardian, New Scientist, USA Today, and ABC Australia, and reached the front page of Reddit.

He appeared on the Planetary Society Radio podcast — “Why is Mars red?” — which drew roughly 500,000 views across channels, and gives interviews in English, Lithuanian, and beyond.
